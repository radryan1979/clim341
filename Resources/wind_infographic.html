From Surface Wind to Turbine Power

A three-part data exercise: characterizing site wind, extrapolating to hub height, and calculating available energy from a commercial turbine.

Part 1 · Site Overview

What is a wind resource assessment?

Before building a wind turbine, an energy company must characterize the wind at the proposed site — how fast it blows, how variable it is, and when it occurs. This drives the engineering and financial case for the project.

Mt. Tom is a ridge in western Massachusetts with persistent winds due to its elevation above surrounding terrain — a classic inland wind resource site in the northeastern U.S.

Parts 1 & 2 · Wind Profile Theory

The logarithmic wind profile (log law)

Meteorological stations measure wind at 2–3 m above the surface — but turbine hubs sit at 40–100 m. Wind speed increases with height due to surface friction. The log law approximates this vertical profile:

U(z) = Ustn × ln(z / z₀) / ln(zstn / z₀)
SymbolMeaningValue used
zTarget height60 m, then 100 m
zstnSensor height2.5 m
z₀Surface roughness lengthSite-dependent (table)

Surface roughness z₀ varies by terrain — open farmland (~0.03 m), scrubland (~0.1 m), forest (~0.5 m). Students select from a provided reference table.

Part 2 · Turbine Operations

Cut-in and cut-out wind speeds

A wind turbine cannot operate at all wind speeds. Below the cut-in speed there is too little energy; above the cut-out speed structural damage becomes a risk.

Students calculate what percentage of the month's data falls within the operational window. The Gamesa G126-2.625 MW has a wider range (1–25 m/s), potentially boosting capacity factor.

Part 2 · Power Calculation

Wind power equation

The power available in moving air depends on its density, the area swept by the rotor blades, and the cube of the wind speed — meaning small increases in wind speed yield large increases in power.

P = ½ × ρ × A × U³
ρ = 1.225 kg/m³ · A = πr² · U in m/s → P in Watts
ρ
Air density
A
Swept area
U³
Speed cubed

Gamesa G126-2.625 MW: rotor diameter 126 m → r = 63 m → A = π × 63² ≈ 12,469 m². Students plot the power curve from 1–25 m/s in 0.5 m/s steps to see how rapidly P grows with U.

Part 3 · Energy Estimation

Combining the power curve with wind frequency to get total energy

Step 1 · Bin the wind dataSort hub-height wind speeds (100 m) into narrow bins (0.5 m/s wide). Count how many 10-minute intervals fall in each bin to produce a wind frequency distribution.

Step 2 · Compute bin energyFor each bin i, multiply the power P(Ui) by the total time at that speed: Δti = count × 10 min. Convert W·min → kWh.

Ei = P(Ui) × Δti

Step 3 · Sum over all binsTotal monthly energy is the sum across all N bins. Units: W × s → J → kWh via conversion factors.

E = Σ Ei   (i = 1 to N)
Part 3 · Diurnal Analysis

Average wind by hour of day

Wind often follows a diurnal cycle — speeds can peak in the afternoon (daytime heating drives convection and turbulent mixing) and drop overnight. Plotting mean wind speed for each hour, averaged over the dataset, reveals this pattern.

Key Concepts Summary

What students learn across all three parts

①
Wind statistics — mean, σ, extremes characterize a site's resource quality.
②
Log-law extrapolation — surface measurements must be adjusted to hub height before use.
③
Operational limits — only a fraction of all wind speeds produce usable power.
④
P ∝ U³ — the cube relationship means site wind speed is the dominant variable.
⑤
Energy = Power × Time — binning wind data enables realistic monthly energy estimates.
⑥
Diurnal cycles — wind resources are not constant; timing patterns matter for grid integration.
