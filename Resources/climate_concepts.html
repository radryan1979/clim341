Part 1

What is a Climate Anomaly?

Here's a simple question: Was last July unusually hot?

To answer that, you can't just look at the temperature and say "it was 31°C." That tells you it was warm — but was it unusually warm? Warmer than a typical July? That's exactly what a climate anomaly tells you.

An anomaly is the difference between what you actually observed and what is "normal" for that time of year — where "normal" means the long-term average.

The anomaly formula Anomaly = Observed Temperature − Long-Term Average

If the result is positive, it was warmer than usual. If it's negative, it was cooler. A result near zero means it was right on par.

Figure 1. Computing a July temperature anomaly. The observed value (31°C) minus the long-term July average (26°C) gives a +5°C warm anomaly.

Why do scientists use anomalies instead of raw temperatures?

Good question. You might wonder: why not just plot the actual temperatures? The reason is that raw temperatures are hard to compare across locations, and they're dominated by the seasonal cycle (summers are hot, winters are cold — that's not news). Anomalies solve these problems.

Figure 2. Raw temperatures in Alaska and Arizona look completely different. But both cities experienced the same +4°C anomaly — meaning both were equally "unusual" relative to their own norms. Anomalies make this comparison possible.

The baseline: what counts as "normal"?

The long-term average is computed over a defined baseline period — typically 30 years. The World Meteorological Organization currently uses 1991–2020 as the standard baseline. Every anomaly you see in a climate report is measured against this reference window.

Key idea

An anomaly is always relative to its baseline. If you change the baseline, the anomaly values change too. That's why scientists always state which baseline they're using.

What anomalies look like: the classic red-blue chart

Climate scientists visualize anomalies using a bar chart where red bars mean warmer than average and blue bars mean cooler than average. This is one of the most recognized charts in climate science.

Part 2

What is Correlation?

Suppose you notice something about the weather in your town: months with a lot of rain also tend to be cooler. And hot, dry months seem to go together. Is that a real pattern, or just a coincidence?

Correlation is the statistical tool that answers this question. It measures whether two variables tend to move together — and if so, how strongly and in what direction.

The Pearson correlation coefficient r measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables

Result always falls between −1 and +1

Figure 3. The correlation coefficient r runs from −1 (as one variable increases, the other always decreases — points form a perfect downward line) to +1 (both increase together — perfect upward line). Zero means no detectable linear relationship.

Important caveat

⚠️ Correlation Does Not Mean Causation

This is probably the single most important statistical lesson you'll ever learn. Say it with us:

Correlation ≠ Causation

Just because two things tend to move together does not mean one is causing the other. There are three common explanations for a correlation:

Figure 4. Three possible reasons for a correlation. The most common trap is Scenario 3 — a hidden "confounding" variable drives both A and B, making them appear related when they aren't directly linked.

Classic cautionary tale

Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are strongly correlated in the summer. Does ice cream cause drowning? Of course not. Both are driven by hot weather — the hidden third variable. In climate science, always ask: could a third factor (like season, geography, or a large-scale circulation pattern) be driving both variables at once?

In climate science, finding a correlation is the beginning of an investigation, not the end. You also need:

1. A physical mechanism — can you explain how one variable could physically cause or influence the other?
2. Statistical significance — is the correlation strong enough that it's unlikely to be random chance, especially with short datasets?
3. Consideration of confounders — could a third variable (like season, geography, or El Niño) be driving both?
